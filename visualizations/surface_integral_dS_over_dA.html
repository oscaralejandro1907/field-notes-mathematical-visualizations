Why a surface integral is more than its shadow

To integrate over a curved surface, we slice it into tiny patches \(dS\) and add up the contributions. The trick is that each patch lives in 3D, but we want to integrate over the flat domain \(D\) we can actually parameterize. So we relate each patch to the shadow it casts straight down onto the plane, the little rectangle \(dA = dx\,dy\).

A tilted patch always has more area than its shadow. If the surface is the graph \(z = f(x,y)\), the exact stretch factor is the length of the (un-normalized) normal vector:

$$\mathbf{n} = \big(-f_x,\;-f_y,\;1\big), \qquad \lVert\mathbf{n}\rVert = \sqrt{1 + f_x^{2} + f_y^{2}}$$ the steeper the surface, the longer the normal

That length is precisely the factor by which area is magnified going from the flat shadow up to the slanted patch — equivalently \(1/\cos\theta\), where \(\theta\) is the tilt of the patch away from horizontal:

$$dS \;=\; \sqrt{1 + f_x^{2} + f_y^{2}}\;dA \;=\; \frac{dA}{\cos\theta} \;=\; \lVert\mathbf{n}\rVert\,dA$$ the orange patch vs. its orange shadow in the diagram

Substituting this into the integral turns an awkward integral over a curved surface into an ordinary double integral over the flat region \(D\) — something we know how to compute:

$$\iint_{S} g(x,y,z)\,dS \;=\; \iint_{D} g\big(x,\,y,\,f(x,y)\big)\,\sqrt{1 + f_x^{2} + f_y^{2}}\;dA$$ the master formula for a scalar surface integral

When \(g \equiv 1\), the integral just returns the area of the surface itself. Everything hinges on that one stretch factor \(\sqrt{1 + f_x^{2} + f_y^{2}}\) — the bridge between the patch and its shadow.
